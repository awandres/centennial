A New Century ... A Global Industry ... A Global Association

As the industry moved into a new century, it was full of changes, challenges, and opportunities. Industry consolidation continued as financiers became major industry players. Increasing real estate values, the maturation of the family entertainment center sector, and financial problems of several operators led to a wave of park closings. This included nearly two dozen seaside amusement parks in the United States and England, along with major facilities in Australia, Europe, and Japan.

But there were still many positive aspects of the decade. After two decades of failed attempts by other operators, the attractions industry started to take root in emerging markets like China and the Middle East. While the latest roller coaster arms race ended, technology started to play an increasing role as suppliers and facilities started developing attractions that provided a more complete experience

2000 | IAAPA launched the “International Year of the Carousel,” a fundraiser for Make-A-Wish, along with a series of press events around the world celebrating one of the industry’s most iconic attractions.

James H. Drew Exposition brought the Seattle Wheel, a unique Ferris wheel built by Chance Rides in 1962 for the Seattle World’s Fair, to the IAAPA trade show in Atlanta. Rides were offered for $2 each with proceeds going to Make-A-Wish and Give Kids The World.

The trade show ambassador program was launched with 21 students assisting in the operation of the convention. The program stemmed from IAAPA’s participation in a course at George Washington University in 1999 that sent colleges to professional conferences to record and write summaries of education sessions.

2001 | IAAPA introduced its current logo, reflecting a more global association.

Following the announced retirement of longtime President and CEO John Graff, IAAPA launched a transition process that began in May with the hiring of trade association executive Bret Lovejoy as incoming president. Graff remained on the job through 2001 to ease in the transition.

The first international office opened in Brussels on Aug. 1. With 800 members, Europe represented the largest international region for the organization. Jeff Bertus, head of the European parks association Europarks, was named IAAPA Vice President, Europe.

The lobbying firm Williams & Jensen was hired to make the industry a more visible presence in the U.S. capital.

Following the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks on the United States, the Summer Meeting at Cedar Point was canceled. While a record 400 were expected, about 30 made it to the park, along with a handful of IAAPA staffers, and they were treated to special tours and social events courtesy of Cedar Point.

2002 | Bret Lovejoy tendered his resignation effective June 28, and the board appointed industry veteran Clark Robinson as interim president. Robinson spent much of his career at Lagoon Amusement Park in Utah, served as IAAPA president in 1981, and served on numerous board and committee positions. Robinson initially took over for six months, but ended up staying three and a half years as his appointment was made permanent in November.

IAAPA’s global profile grew throughout the year. The World Council held its first dedicated meeting in Brussels; IAAPA Europe hostedits first official seminar at Efteling in Kaatsheuvel, Netherlands; and Ashok Goel was appointed to the IAAPA Board of Directors, the first director from India. The organization also won a seat on London-based World Travel & Tourism Council (WTTC).

John Graff remained with the association in the role of director of the charities program to direct efforts to support three organizations—Give Kids The World and two new additions, UNICEF and the International Institute for Peace Through Tourism.

2003 | A brain injury study commissioned by Six Flags Inc. and supported by IAAPA was released. Conducted by the American Association of Neurological Surgeons and Exponent Failure Analysis Associates, it refuted media and political claims linking thrill rides to brain injuries. A separate report by the Brain Injury Association of America’s Blue-Ribbon Panel, which was convened at the requestof U.S. Rep. Edward Markey, an advocate of increased federal oversight of the industry, came to the same conclusion. It went so far as to state: “It is unlikely that any federal agency could match the amusement park industry’s self-monitoring.”

The industry’s commitment to safety grew as IAAPA introduced an incident reporting database and launched the International Standards Harmonization Group to bring consistency to safety standards throughout the world.

IAAPA acquired the 12-year-old Asian Amusement Exposition from co-owners Terrapinn Pte. Ltd. and the American Amusement Machine Association. IAAPA was a longtime supporter of the show, and the purchase permitted the association to add an educational component. Unfortunately, the 2003 show ended up being canceled due to the SARS virus that spread through southern Asia.

The IAAPA trade show moved into the new North/South building at the Orange County Convention Center and was the first major Expo in the facility. It has become the long-term home to the conference and trade show, held there all but three times since. In addition, the IAAPA Bookstore debuted at the convention.

The annual charity golf tournament teed off in support of Give Kids The World, the first of several events that are now held to support the charity during IAAPA Attractions Expo week.

2004 | The association changed the name of the annual convention and trade show to IAAPA Attractions Expo to help distinguish the event from the numerous other activities of the organization.

New governance bylaws reduced the size of the board from 55 to 28 members.

IAAPA and European Association Amusement Supplier Industry (EAASI) signed a formal agreement to form a new company that would own and manage the Euro Amusements Show for 10 years. It was rebranded as the Euro Attractions Show (EAS) in 2006. IAAPA would purchase full ownership in 2007.

IAAPA adopted a new Mission and Vision Statement:

MISSION STATEMENT

Our mission is to serve the membership by promoting safe operations, global development, professional growth, and commercial success of the amusement parks and attractions industry.

VISION

We envision a professional association regarded as an indispensable resource for our members and an international authority for our industry. Central to achieving our vision is a focus on our members as the reason for our existence and a resolute respect for our employees as the source of our strength.

2005 | The World Council and International Representatives programs were transitioned into the Global Alliances initiative. Under the program, national, regional, and state associations would act as allies, providing direct service to IAAPA members in their languages and home markets. In exchange, IAAPA would provide benefits ranging from educational programs and resources support to limited financial funding.

The first annual Ride Safety Report was released, reporting incident data submitted by IAAPA members in the United States.

The IAAPA Museum Committee was established to better address the needs of this emerging constituency. This process began in 2003 when two education sessions were presented at the trade show. A task force was formed in 2004, leading to the committee’s creation.

The Directory and Buyers Guide, published since 1944, was transitioned to a digital resource guide on IAAPA’s website.

IAAPA took over production of the Show Daily newspaper from Amusement Business (AB); the IAAPA Attractions Expo publication was launched by AB in the late 1980s.

IAAPA launched the Oral History Project to interview industry veterans and legends, and record their memories to educate and inspire the next generation of attractions industry leaders. These interviews will start becoming available as part of the association’s centennial celebration.

2006 | Clark Robinson announced his retirement as president and CEO, and was replaced by career trade association executive Charlie Bray, who was hired as IAAPA’s first chief financial officer in 2004.

The ride incident reporting system was made mandatory for U.S. members with qualified rides.

To better support the growing family entertainment center (FEC) constituency, IAAPA teamed with American Specialty Insurance to launch F.I.R.S.T. (FEC Insurance, Risk Management, Service, Training). A multifaceted program, it offered insurance, training programs, safety inspections, and web-based claims reporting and safety alerts; the association offers a variation on this program today.

Casinos were invited to become members for the first time, acknowledging these facilities’ short-lived transition into heavily themed environments with attractions designed to lure gamblers into the resorts.

2007 | IAAPA revamped and relocated its annual senior-level training program, with the 15th edition of the newly renamed IAAPA Institute for Executive Education at the Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania in Philadelphia. Later that year, at IAAPA Attractions Expo, the association unveiled its new Institute for Emerging Leaders to help foster industry professionals with at least three years’ management experience whose skill base made them possible candidates for senior-level positions (now called the IAAPAInstitute for Attractions Managers).

IAAPA acquired full ownership of the Euro Attractions Show, and IAAPA Attractions Expo moved to its current Tuesday-through-Friday schedule, eliminating the Saturday operation.

2008 | The inaugural European Ride Safety Report was released in cooperation with Europarks.In February, Andreas Andersen of Copenhagen’s Tivoli Gardens was hired as the new Executive Director, IAAPA European Operations, to support an enhanced presence in Europe. This included a more robust safety strategy, increased government relations, training, a newweb portal, and the IAAPA Europe Newsletter.

Continuing a long-term dedication to safety, the association also held its first IAAPA Safety Conference in the Middle East in Dubai in February. In June, it conducted an Attractions Safety Awareness Week in partnership with its members to enhance governmental and public understanding of the industry’s safety practices and outstanding record.

Two European trade shows were held as part of a plan to transition the Euro Attractions Show from a winter to a fall event—one in Nice,France, in January and a second in Munich in October.

The Summer Meeting, a tradition since 1949, was held for the final time at Oktoberfest in Munich prior to EAS.

IAAPA hired industry veteran Ben Jones of RECreation Inc. to serve as the association’s first family entertainment center (FEC) specialist, dedicated solely to supplying outreach, support, and assistance to the association’s FEC community.

The association launched IAAPA News Flash, a daily e-mail compilation of industry news from around the world sent directly to members’ inboxes. Within a year it was published more than 125 days a year and was available in 11 languages.

2009 | The International Association for the Leisure and Entertainment Industry (IALEI), a trade association representing the FEC industry, reached an agreement to dissolve and merge its membership into IAAPA. Enhanced services to the FEC constituency included a dedicated staff member and an FEC internet portal.

The association established the IAAPA Latin America regional office in Mexico City, with industry and association veteran Paulina Reyes as executive director.

IAAPA Europe launched the IAAPA Europe Insurance Program, completed the first European Economic Impact Study, and made the Ride Incident Reporting System mandatory for European members with qualified rides.

Enhanced training efforts through an online webinar series were part of the new Partnership Plus program on the website. Other website features included offering back issues of Funworld magazine and providing archives of handouts from expo educational sessions.

IAAPA Attractions Expo was held in Las Vegas as what was originally envisioned as a new East-West rotation of the Expo. Plans to return in 2011 were changed due to challenges with the facility, logistics, and costs, and IAAPA committed to remain in Orlando through 2019 (an agreement that has since been extended).
