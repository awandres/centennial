Going Global

The industry evolved on many levels during the 1990s. The opening of Disneyland Paris in France led to a wave of new development throughout Europe. A new roller coaster arms race, launched at the end of the 1980s, was a global phenomenon throughout the decade as larger and larger rides were built and new genres such as the inverted coaster created new thrills for riders all over the world. With consumers increasingly pressed for time, family entertainment centers came into their own by offering a limited number of attractions that could be enjoyed in a few hours.

But likely the most transformative event of the 1990s was the investment banker-driven consolidation wave that spread throughout the world. Premier Parks led the surge, acquiring family-owned facilities, injecting new life into their purchases with heavy investment. Premier’s growth culminated in its acquisition of Six Flags in 1998 and eventual expansion into Europe. Other companies followed suit, and by the end of the decade many family-owned parks were operating under corporate umbrellas.

1990 | IAAPA continued to increase its international engagement:

A seminar held in Tokyo on amusement park development, in cooperation with the Japan Development Bank, attracted more than 100 people.

IAAPA teamed with Amusement Business to host a reception at the Interschau trade show in Germany.

An IAAPA delegation traveled to the Soviet Union to meet with leaders of the Soviet Ministry of Culture.

An agreement was signed with Europarks, the European trade association, in which IAAPA could utilize Europarks’ offices on a part-time basis to better serve IAAPA’s European members.

Meanwhile, Europarks would provide lobbying representation to European IAAPA members.AMAP (the Association of Mexican Amusement Parks) invited IAAPA to conduct a daylong safety seminar that attracted 120 attendees. The seminar reprised later in the year in Brazil.

A larger International Council was established.

To better assist members, IAAPA produced its first training video. Focusing on safety, it was followed with another on new employee orientation.

The IAAPA Hall of Fame inducted its inaugural class, recognizing pioneers in the industry.

1991 | IAAPA launched the sister parks program linking a U.S. member facility with a similar one outside the U.S. The intent was to giveattractions an opportunity to share information, exchange employees and ideas, and develop international camaraderie.

A speaker’s bureau was launched to link industry experts with interested parties.

The headquarters moved into its longtime home in Alexandria, Virginia, with available space increasing from 4,000 to 8,000 square feet.

Seeking to provide members with increased educational and training resources, the association hired Lisa Hunnewell as its first director of education. During the year, the organization produced two educational videos and held three educational seminars.

The convention returned to Orlando to find a convention center expanded to 350,000 net square feet, more than double the size in 1986. There were no tents.

Membership passed 3,000 for the first time.

Bo Kinntorph of Liseberg in Sweden became the first chairman of the board from outside the United States. His acceptance speech took the form of a poem he had the banquet read along with him:

Presidents come and presidents go

They always want yes and never a no

The people behind stand by to support

With excellence, charm, and a lot of good sport

And we all, the members, give thanks to the staff

So brilliantly managed by our John Graff

Let’s toast to our office and finally say

Yippee-I-A-A-P-A-Hooray!

1992 | Educational programs were increasing. An on-site training program was launched, which sent industry experts directly to facilities, while international educational seminars were held in Paris, at Interschau in Germany, and in Japan.

IAAPA increased services to address the needs of the emerging family entertainment center (FEC) sector. An FEC committee was created, and a daylong workshop for FEC development and operation was held at the convention.

The organization held a Future of the Industry Conference in Williamsburg, Virginia, in July to identify emerging trends and strategize how the industry would evolve to reflect those changes.

1993 | After two years of planning, IAAPA launched the Amusement Institute Program for senior management through Cornell University’s School of Hotel Administration. The first session in January attracted 27 participants from eight countries.

The Summer Meeting was held outside the U.S. for the first time, in Gothenburg, Sweden. Hosted by Liseberg, a highlight was a Viking-themed banquet, where a food fight broke out, the origins of which are now part of IAAPA legend.

In honor of the association’s 75th anniversary, the conference and trade show traveled to Los Angeles for the first time. It shattered previous records with nearly 25,000 registrants, including 1,000 employees from The Walt Disney Company. The event received unprecedented media coverage, including on CNN and in USA Today. However, cost and logistical issues made this a one-time location, and a plan to return in 1996 was shifted to New Orleans. The IAAPA staff totaled 15 people.

1994 | IAAPA created the Manufacturers and Sales Committee (MSC) to give industry suppliers a greater voice in the operation of the association, and it added a staff member to act as a liaison.

A resource library was launched. Consisting of a collection of industry-related books, articles, publications, and videotapes, along with materials related to management, training, and legislation, members could have items mailed to them for two-week loans.

The Training the Trainer educational seminar was inaugurated with sessions in Mexico and Virginia.

The board of directors approved a plan for IAAPA to become an active supporter of Give Kids The World. In addition to in-kind support, the agreement invited members to participate in a World Passport for Kids Program providing free admission to all participating attractions.

1995 | IAAPA started the year by “merging onto the information superhighway.” At the time, there was not a website, but rather an e-mail address, IAAPA@aol.com, that permitted members to more effectively communicate with the main office.

A recommendation of the International Council, a program consisting of 10 park employees from throughout the world, formed an international ambassadors group. These 10 would serve as the eyes and ears of IAAPA in their respective regions.

IAAPA made its first foray into the Middle East, conducting a three-day seminar and trade exhibition in Dubai.

IAAPA teamed with the American Amusement Machine Association (AAMA) to present the second Asian Amusement Expo in Hong Kong in June. It attracted 1,769 attendees and 73 exhibitors.

After five years of study, the board of directors voted to accept a 13-step, long-range plan developed by the International Council that would give the association a stronger relevance to all international members. Goals included further delineating the world into regions overseen by regional representatives, improvements in translation services, worldwide safety standards, arbitration guidelines, communications improvements, globalization of government relations, increased board representation, fee revisions, improving the Sister Park program, and expansion of training programs.

1996 | IAAPA launched its first website. Features included the member directory and buyers guide, a directory of staff, products and services, a chatroom, forums, and an online trading post.

The association purchased an adjoining office building at the headquarters in Alexandria, Virginia, to nearly double the size of its existing space and accommodate a staff that had grown to more than 20.

With strong input from Chair Geoffrey Thompson, the organization kicked off the “International Year of the Roller Coaster,” its first worldwide publicity program.

Gina Romano of Nellie Bly Amusement Park in Brooklyn, New York, became the association’s first female president (now chair).

IAAPA introduced the Amusement Facility Management School (now IAAPA Institute of Attractions Managers) at the convention.

1997 | IAAPA and AAMA combined their Asian Amusement Expo with AIC Exhibitions’ Asia Pacific Theme Parks & Attractions Conference & Exhibition to create what they hoped would be the definitive event in Asia for exhibitors and buyers; this event is now known as Asian Attractions Expo which is wholly owned by IAAPA.

As a result of the recommendations by the planning committee, IAAPA implemented a leadership structure in which the staff was given more autonomy in running day-to-day operations, with the board focusing more on strategic issues. The executive director’s title was changed to president and CEO, and the board president became the chairman. In addition, the board added the position of treasurer.

The conference and trade show returned to Orlando, and, for the first time, the number of exhibitors passed 1,000 and registration passed 30,000.

1998 | IAAPA held a series of four Future of the Industry conferences in Canada, Argentina, Sweden, and Australia to allow members to learn about emerging trends that were impacting the industry. The seminars were intended to help in the development of the next strategic plan for the association.

The National Amusement Park Historical Association (NAPHA) partnered with IAAPA to launch an oral history project. NAPHA members completed about a dozen interviews that helped support a “Legends of the Industry” series in Funworld.

In an effort to increase IAAPA’s profile on a variety of issues important to the attractions industry, including safety, IAAPA attractions the role of public relations coordinator to focus on external communications and develop proactive relationships with the press. This marked the beginning of IAAPA’s multifaceted communications department.

The trade show in Dallas featured nearly 1,100 exhibitors across almost 500,000 net square feet of space, including 50,000 net square feet outside. Exhibitors were housed in two levels of exhibit halls, an arena, and a ballroom. It was evident the trade show had outgrown all but a handful of convention centers.

1999 | IAAPA added a membership committee and continued to expand its constituency groups by creating a zoo and aquarium committee.

IAAPA launched Asian Amusement Management School, a three-day educational seminar with 40 hours of instruction covering human resources, safety, finance, and operations.

The 1999 conference and trade show in Atlanta broke 500,000 net square feet of exhibit space for the first time, including 45,000 net square feet in the Georgia Dome (taking the place of outdoor exhibits). The show also saw the debut of the IAAPA Big E awards to honor the best in live entertainment and the Spirit of Excellence Awards to recognize best human resources practices. Both of these programs were eventually merged into the IAAPA Brass Ring Awards program.

Rene Aziz of Mexico’s Grupo Magico became the first board chair from Latin America.
